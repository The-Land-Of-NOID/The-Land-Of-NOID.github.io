Picking Locks (Interpretation)

Back to House Rules and Miscellaneous

 

There is some confusion as to how Picking Locks works. This is my simplified interpretation:

 

       Anyone can attempt to open a lock if they use Thieves Tools and make a successful DEX check.

       If you are Proficient with Thieves Tools, you can add your Proficiency Bonus to the DEX check. (If you dont get Proficiency some other way, you can get it by taking the Skilled Feat).

       If you have Expertise with Thieves Tools, you can add double your Proficiency Bonus to the DEX check. (If you dont get Expertise some other way, you can get it by taking the Skill Expert Feat to improve an existing Proficiency with Thieves Tools).

 

No Thieves Tools? You need SOME kind of tool. If you come up with a substitute that the DM allows, you can make the lockpicking attempt at Disadvantage. If you dont have anything acceptable, all thats left is magic or brute force. Depending on what youre breaking into, you may risk harming whatever the lock is protecting.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Spent Arrows (House Rules)

Back to House Rules and Miscellaneous

 

When an arrow (or crossbow bolt) hits a body, it sticks there until removed. Pulling it out without due caution ruins the arrow and causes 1d4 damage on top of the initial damage. Leaving the arrow in causes 1d4 damage per day until removed.

 

If your goal is to remove an arrow from a body, causing no more harm, make a WIS (or Medicine) check (DC10):

       Natural 20: No additional damage to the body. The arrow comes out undamaged.

       Success: No additional damage to the body. The arrow is ruined.

       Failure: Body takes 1d2 damage. The arrow is ruined.

Using a knife or other pointy tool gives Advantage. Time required: 1 minute.

 

If your goal is to recover the arrow, roll a DEX (or Sleight of Hand) check (DC 10):

       Natural 20: Arrow comes out undamaged. No further damage to the body.

       Success: Arrow comes out undamaged. Body takes 1d2 damage.

       Failure: Arrow is ruined. Body takes 1d4 damage.

Using a knife or other pointy tool gives Advantage. Time required: 1 Action.

 

If multiple arrows are involved, the player may opt to make a single roll for all or nothing instead of making a bunch of individual rolls. Arrow removal takes the normal amount of time this just saves dice rolling time.

A character can use an Action to attempt to rip out an opponents arrow (one at a time) to cause him harm. To grab an arrow, make an unarmed attack against your opponents unarmored Armor Class (they get any DEX bonus). If you hit, you rip out an arrow (automatically ruining it) and they take 1d4 damage. A critical hit causes 2d4 damage.

 

When an arrow misses its target, it is most likely unable to be recovered. Indoors it breaks against walls, and outdoors it flies off, getting lost. Roll 1d6. On a 6, the arrow can be recovered. (You may roll your recovery check immediately after a miss, and return it to your quiver on a success. That way you only need to track how many were hits). Arrows that pass through noncorporeal targets (like ghosts) are considered to have missed with regard to recovery, even if they did damage.

On occasion, you may face an opponent that is well armored (such as a dragon). In these instances, an arrow may cause damage but bounce off the target. Treat these hits as misses with regard to arrow recovery.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hide (House Rules)

Back to House Rules and Miscellaneous

 

For the sake of clarity, we will use the term "Hider" when describing someone who is hiding, and "Seeker" for those who have a chance to notice them (even though they might not be actively looking).

 

When a character takes the HIDE action, he attempts to be unseen from others. He cannot hide from anyone that has him under direct observation at the beginning of the round. He must be in an area where he can become Heavily Obscured. A heavily obscured area - such as darkness, opaque fog, or dense foliage - blocks vision entirely.

 

To detect a Hidden character, an Alert Seeker must succeed on a Perception (WIS) check vs. the Hider's Stealth (DEX) roll. A Seeker is considered to be alert if hes in combat, standing guard, scouting, infiltrating an enemy stronghold, etc. In other words, he has reason to believe there may be foes around. He can declare he is being alert for any reason, but he cannot remain actively alert if he moves more than speed.

 

An Unwary Seeker is anyone who is not alert. They suffer Disadvantage on their Perception (WIS) check to match the Hider's Stealth (DEX) roll. (The guidebooks say to use the Seeker's Passive Perception, but I prefer the more active use of Disadvantage).

 

This opposed ability check is made once, when the Dungeon Master determines that the possibility for detection exists. This is typically when the Seeker's attention is in the Hider's general direction and they are within a range of no more than the Hider's Passive Perception (x10) feet. A second check is only called for if conditions change (the Hider moves, or makes a sound greater than a whisper, etc).

 

The DM will often make both rolls in secret in order to keep character's actions from being influenced by the player's knowledge of the result. As far as the Hider is concerned, he believes he's concealed. But a Seeker may not always want to give away the fact that they have detected the Hider for whatever reason.

 

A successful Hider that attacks an alert Seeker from hiding has Advantage on their first attack. (Not all their attacks, if they get more than one; just the opening attack). If the Seeker was unwary, then the Seeker is Surprised. Surprised characters can do nothing the whole first round of combat until the very end, when they can only use a Reaction (if they have one) and communicate. A character who attacks is no longer hidden.

 

There are certain minor exceptions to the basic rules for Hiding:

       The "Skulker" Feat allows you to Hide while only Lightly Obscured.

       A Wood Elf has "Mask of the Wild", an ability to Hide while only Lightly Obscured.

       A Lightfoot Halfling is "Naturally Stealthy", so can Hide behind any creature of Medium size or larger.

       A Ranger can get the class feat "Hide in Plain Sight" that allows them to camouflage themselves and hide up against a solid surface of at least the same size.

 

Working with Groups

If dealing with an entire group that is Hiding or Seeking, then the situation is resolved a little differently. A group makes its detection roll using a representative. The Hider with the lowest Stealth skill is that group's Weakest Link. He is the most likely to be spotted, so if he goes unnoticed then so does everyone else. The Seeker with the highest Perception skill is that group's Best Spotter. If the he notices something, then he can alert his companions about Hiding foes, ruining any ambush bonuses they might have had. This all-or-nothing process may seem a little unrealistic or unfair at times, but it is a time saver and confusion reducer, where otherwise it could be quite a chore remembering who notices who amongst even small groups.

 

Whenever less than half of a group is attempting to Hide, the individuals use their personal Stealth checks vs. the opposing Seeker or Best Spotter. Obviously, all Seekers will be alert if there are unconcealed Hiders around.

 

If a group of Seekers contains some members that are Alert and others that are unwary, the Alert character with the highest Perception score is their Best Spotter, even if an unwary character has a higher score.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Holding Your Action (House Rule)

Back to House Rules and Miscellaneous

 

If for any reason you want to hold off doing anything when your turn comes up, you can wait until you do want to do something.  When you decide that the time is ripe, you can act as normal, but this point in the Initiative sequence becomes your new Initiative.

Example: Roger the Rogue rolls a 17 Initiative.  On his turn, there aren't any enemies in his view.  He knows they're nearby, but he doesn't want to leave his hiding place.  So he decides to wait.  A bit later, a particularly nasty Kobold - Initiative 12 - runs into the room.  Roger decides it's time to act.  So he jumps out and makes his attacks.  He now has an Initiative of 12 for the remainder of the combat (unless he decides to wait again).

If you are still waiting when the combat round ends, then your Initiative for the new round is the same as it was when you began waiting.  You cannot improve your Initiative by waiting until the beginning of the next round and deciding to act then.  Your Initiative can only drop by waiting: your Actions from one combat round to the next must be spaced no closer together than 6 seconds.  Some players may decide it's better to essentially skip their turn and retain their high Initiative.

This replaces what D&D 5e calls Ready an Action. Essentially, you are giving up your rolled Initiative and taking a lower score.

Sometimes, in determining how hard a task is, the DM will take into account the characters background. If what he is trying to accomplish is something that hes trained to do something he does all the time the DC may be lowered. For example, if Joe Average buys a set of Thieves Tools, he has a DC 15 to open the average lock. But a Rogue or a Locksmith does that for a living, so their DC would become a 10. Childs play!

A natural roll of 20 always succeeds, even if the total doesnt meet the target DC. A natural roll of 1 always fails. A natural 1 while attempting a Very Hard or Nearly Impossible task has the possibility of some negative result.


 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Resting (House Rules)

Back to House Rules and Miscellaneous

 

The rules for resting are a little fuzzy, and a little more complicated than they need to be. Were going to use almost the same system, but simplified bit. You need to think of Hit Points as fatigue rather than injury. Rest can restore most or even all of a characters lost Hit Points every day.

 

SHORT REST

A short rest is a period of downtime at least 1 hour long, during which a character does nothing more strenuous than eating, drinking, reading, and tending to wounds. Whatever your character has for hit dice is what he has for RECOVERY dice each day. For example, if hes an 8th level Fighter with a 16 Constitution, then he has 8d10 hit dice, with an additional 3 points per die (24) for his CON bonus. So after a short rest, he can roll 1d10(+3) up to 8 times, and recover that many hit points. If he rolls 3 of his 8 dice (+CON), he has 5 remaining for the day. If 3 dice wasnt enough, he can roll more sets of 1d10(+3) until hes satisfied. Any unused Recovery dice can be used later in the day after taking another short rest. Multi-Classed characters have Recovery dice of whatever number and type each individual class has. Recovery dice regenerate at Sunrise each day.

LONG REST

A long rest is a period of extended downtime at least 8 hours long, during which a character sleeps for at least 6 hours and performs no more than 2 hours of light activity (such as reading, talking, eating, or standing watch). At the end of a long rest, a character regains all lost hit points and spell points. If the rest is interrupted by a period of strenuous activity - fighting, casting spells, or similar adventuring activity - the characters must begin the rest again to gain any benefit from it. As in real life, you can still get a full nights sleep but not be fully rested. Exposure to harsh environments, for example, can be one reason why you might not fully recover after a sleep period. You can only receive the benefits of a Long Rest once every 24-hour period.

 

The benefits of resting only apply AFTER the rest is completed. Dont be too quick to restore your character until its certain the rest wont be interrupted.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Free Actions (Clarification / House Rules)

Back to House Rules and Miscellaneous

 

Free Actions are things you can do in addition to your normal Movement and Action allowance. Its important for players to understand everything they are allowed to do, so they dont shortchange themselves.

First and foremost amongst the Free Actions is talking. As a House Rule, you are allowed to speak at any point during the combat round, as long as what you say doesnt exceed 6 seconds. I have a timer. Ill give you a moment to compose what youd like to say, then Ill let you say it. If you think you can squeeze it all in, Ill even let you break up your speech just like your movement, as long as the total time remains no longer than 6 seconds.

As a Free Action, you may also make brief interactions with objects around you. You may only make one such interaction on your turn. For example:

 

       Draw or sheathe a sword

       Open or close a door

       Withdraw a potion from your backpack

       Pick up a dropped axe

       Take a bauble from a table

       Remove a ring from your finger

       Stuff some food into your mouth

       Plant a banner in the ground

       Fish a few coins from your belt pouch

       Drink all the ale in a flagon

       Throw a lever or a switch

       Pull a torch from a sconce

       Take a book from a shelf you can reach

       Extinguish a small flame

       Don a mask

       Pull the hood of your cloak up and over your head

       Put your ear to a door

       Kick a small stone

       Turn a key in a lock

       Tap the floor with a 10-foot pole

       Hand an item to another character

 

Other things here and there may be defined as Free Actions as well. Some creatures may have a special attack or ability which doesnt conflict with their normal Action. The important thing to take away from this, is that with the exception of communicating, you may only perform one Free Action per combat round. So really, the basic turn can include Movement up to your maximum distance, 1 Action, 1 Free Action, and up to 6 seconds of communication.
